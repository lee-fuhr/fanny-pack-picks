How to Choose a Fanny Pack

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose a fanny pack, start by deciding what you’ll carry, your phone, wallet, keys, plus maybe a snack or sunscreen, then pick a size, material, and carry style that fits that load and your daily life. Waist belts are classic for hands-free convenience; crossbody straps offer easier access and less waist squeeze. Prioritize security features like hidden zippers and RFID linings if you travel in crowds, and choose a durable material, nylon for light weight and water resistance, leather for style, that matches how often you’ll use it.

How big of a fanny pack do you need?

Think of capacity in two buckets: a small pack that just holds a phone, card case, and keys, versus a larger one that can also fit a slim water bottle, sunglasses case, or a child’s small toy. Most packs fall in the 1–3 liter range, enough for an everyday phone and wallet without turning into a belly apron. If you plan to carry a tablet or a paperback, look for a pack whose main compartment is listed as fitting an e-reader or slim tablet, but avoid oversized models that slip or bounce when you walk.

Which materials hold up best?

Nylon and polyester ripstop are the workhorses: light, water-resistant, and easy to wipe clean. Brands like Patagonia and The North Face use these for their outdoor-oriented packs because they hold up year after year. Waxed canvas or coated nylon adds a rugged look and beads off rain, though it can be heavier. Leather fanny packs, like those from Bellroy or Herschel, look polished for nights out but require occasional conditioning and won’t shrug off a downpour. For everyday beater use, a high-denier nylon or Cordura pack is your best bet.

Waist belt vs crossbody, which carry style suits you?

A classic waist belt frees your hands and keeps weight centered around your hips, which is ideal for hiking, concerts, or days you don’t want anything swinging. The down side: if you wear it over a jacket or have a shorter torso, it can ride up and feel tight. A crossbody strap (often included or adjustable) lets you wear the pack at your chest or back, making it easier to access your phone without unbuckling. Many modern fanny packs come with both options, so you can switch between a hip sling and a shoulder bag depending on your outfit or activity.

How do you keep your valuables secure?

Look for a main zipper that has a small hole or loop so you can attach a mini padlock or carabiner, a simple trick that foils grab-and-run thieves. Hidden back pockets or internal zippered compartments let you stash cash or a passport away from casual eyes. Some packs, like those from Pacsafe, feature slash-proof fabric and locking zippers, but they can feel over-engineered for daily use. A simpler approach: choose a pack with two zipper pulls and clip them together with a small locking hairpin or keychain clip. RFID-blocking pockets are useful if you carry contactless cards, but most people are fine without it for everyday errands.

What pocket layout works best?

A single big bucket pocket forces you to dig for your keys, you want at least two or three compartments. The ideal setup: a main compartment for a phone and wallet, a front slip pocket for quick-access items like lip balm or transit pass, and a small back or internal zippered pocket for coins or a backup credit card. Look for a mesh inner pocket that keeps your phone separate and easy to grab without pulling everything out. Brands like Lululemon and Topo Designs design multiple pockets that are easy to organize without being bulky.

Can one fanny pack work for travel and daily life?

Yes, if you pick a medium-size pack (around 2 liters) in a neutral color like black, navy, or olive, and one that converts from hip belt to crossbody. That way it transitions from a sightseeing day bag to an everyday errand pouch. Make sure the strap is adjustable enough to fit over a winter coat or around your waist when you’re just wearing a T-shirt. A pack with a padded back panel adds comfort for all-day wear, and a small luggage pass-through loop lets you slide it onto a suitcase handle during airport layovers.

Frequently asked questions

Are fanny packs still in style in 2025?

Absolutely, they’ve become a standard accessory for both practical and fashion reasons. Luxury brands and outdoor companies both make them, and they’re worn everywhere from commuter trains to festivals. The key is choosing a clean, modern silhouette rather than a bulky 1990s nylon pouch.

What’s the biggest phone that fits in a typical fanny pack?

Most standard fanny packs accommodate a phone up to about 6.7 inches (like a current iPhone Pro Max or Samsung S25 Ultra), but you should check the interior width of the main pocket. If you carry a larger phone with a thick case, look for packs that explicitly list a phone size or have a stretchy side pocket that expands.

Can I wear a fanny pack crossbody if it’s meant for the waist?

Yes, many straps are long enough to sling across your chest. If the strap doesn’t detach or run long enough, you can add an extender or use a carabiner clip to attach an extra strap. Just note that wearing it crossbody puts more weight on one shoulder, so a padded strap is nicer for heavier loads.

Are fanny packs allowed at amusement parks, concerts, or sports stadiums?

Most venues allow small fanny packs, but policies vary. Typically clear bags or tiny pouches (under 6 x 9 inches) are okay. Some stadiums ban any bag larger than a clutch, so check the specific venue’s size rules before you go. When in doubt, bring one that’s small enough to wear without being considered a full-size bag.

How do I clean a fanny pack without ruining it?

For nylon or polyester packs, wipe with a damp cloth and mild soap, then air dry. Avoid machine washing unless the tag says it’s safe, the strap padding can get misshapen. For leather packs, use a leather cleaner and conditioner sparingly. Always test any cleaner on a hidden spot first.

Should I choose a leather or nylon fanny pack for everyday use?

Nylon is lighter, dries fast, and shrugs off rain, making it better for daily carry and active use. Leather looks more refined and ages beautifully, but it’s heavier and needs more care. If you plan to use the pack constantly and in all weather, go with nylon. For occasional dinners or dressier outfits, leather works well.

In shortThe best fanny pack is the one you actually reach for every day, small enough to stay out of the way, organized enough to keep your things findable, and comfortable enough to forget you’re wearing it. Focus on your usual carry, pick a material that suits your climate and lifestyle, and don’t sweat the hype: a well-chosen fanny pack will serve you for years on errands, trips, and adventures alike.
